A tale of overcoming trauma and injustice through spiritual and magical practices

Proverbs 22:6: "Train up a child in the way he should go: and when he is old, he will not depart from it."

Damien Echols was born on December 11, 1974, in West Memphis, Arkansas, USA. He had a difficult childhood, growing up in poverty and facing abuse from his stepfather. Echols was diagnosed with bipolar disorder and spent time in psychiatric hospitals during his teenage years. He became interested in alternative religions and the occult, which led to him being considered a suspect in a high-profile murder case.

Psalm 7:9: "Bring to an end the violence of the wicked and make the righteous secure— you, the righteous God who probes minds and hearts."

In May 1993, Echols, along with Jessie Misskelley Jr. and Jason Baldwin, were arrested and charged with the murders of 8-year-olds Stevie Branch, Michael Moore, and Christopher Byers. The prosecution claimed that the murders were part of a satanic ritual. Echols was 18 at the time and faced the death penalty if convicted.

Proverbs 17:15: "Acquitting the guilty and condemning the innocent— the Lord detests them both."

Despite little evidence linking the three teenagers to the crime, they were found guilty in 1994. Echols was sentenced to death, while Misskelley Jr. and Baldwin were given life sentences. The case gained international attention, with many people believing that the teenagers were wrongfully convicted.

Proverbs 17:17: "A friend loves at all times, and a brother is born for a time of adversity."

Over the years, the case continued to generate controversy and sparked public outcry. Supporters of the West Memphis Three argued that the convictions were based on flimsy evidence and that the prosecution had failed to prove their guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Celebrities, including Eddie Vedder of Pearl Jam, Johnny Depp, and Natalie Maines of the Dixie Chicks, were vocal in their support for the West Memphis Three.

1 Corinthians 13:13: "And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love."

In 2007, DNA testing showed that none of the DNA evidence found at the crime scene matched the DNA of Echols, Misskelley Jr., or Baldwin. This led to the case being reopened, and the West Memphis Three were eventually released in 2011, after spending 18 years in prison. Echols had spent more than a decade on death row.

Isaiah 1:17: "Learn to do right; seek justice. Defend the oppressed. Take up the cause of the fatherless; plead the case of the widow."

Since his release, Echols has become an advocate for criminal justice reform and has spoken out about his experiences in prison. He has also published several books, including his memoir, "Life After Death," which details his experiences leading up to his conviction and his life on death row. He has also written about his interest in spiritual practices and the role that they played in helping him cope with the challenges of prison life.

Ecclesiastes 9:9 "Enjoy life with the wife whom you love, all the days of your vain life that he has given you under the sun, because that is your portion in life and in your toil at which you toil under the sun."

Echols is married to Lorri Davis, who was one of his supporters during his time in prison. They met through a mutual friend and maintained a correspondence while Echols was on death row. Davis was instrumental in helping to secure the release of the West Memphis Three, and the couple married shortly after Echols was released from prison.

Proverbs 19:21 "Many are the plans in a person’s heart, but it is the Lord’s purpose that prevails." This reminds us that although we may have our own plans and ideas for our lives, ultimately it is God's purpose that will prevail.

Echols and Davis currently reside in New York City, where they continue to speak out about the injustice of the criminal justice system. Echols has become an advocate for the abolition of the death penalty and has spoken at various events and conferences on the topic. He has also worked with the Innocence Project, an organization that seeks to exonerate wrongfully convicted individuals.
